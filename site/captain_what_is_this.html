Captain, what is this?

2019.07.27
Savusavu, Fiji

After 15 days on the water, we were eager to get into port. It was the 27th of June, and we'd left Opua, New Zealand on the 12th. We hove-to for the night, waiting for the sunrise before entering Savusavu bay. "Entering a strange harbor at night is for morons and fools," people had told us. We had not always listened to this advice, but they were right. In this particular case, there was no point in arriving early because yachts could not anchor in Fijian waters before checking in, and check-in was only possible after 0800.

At dawn, we pointed Pino's bow towards the island of Vanua Levu, with the wind at our backs. I went below to make some coffee — both of us needed a boost. Devine was in the cockpit, they kept an eye on a large storm cloud ahead. The wind increased as we neared it, but it did not trouble us long. We made our way inside the reef, if offered some protection from the building swells. We could see hills ahead, and caught the perfume of plants. After that, came a sure sign of civilization: low buildings with green and red roofs. As we got nearer, came an even more obvious sign of civilization: cars, small power boats, and people. We had arrived.

When nearing the entrance to Nakama creek at 0900, we hailed the authorities on our VHF radio.

I called them 3 times, no answer. We slowed down, waiting for a reply. The commercial wharf lay ahead, we could see boats on moorings, many were abandoned, others belonged to people we'd seen or met across the Pacific. Eventually, the Fijian authorities replied and asked us about about our trip, and if we had sent a pre-arrival notice. Sending a pre-arrival notice ahead of time was mandatory, unfortunate considering it was about 10 pages too long. We had sent our application ahead by email, although our messages had bounced back, and other cruisers reported the same thing happening. With the document being so large, it was no wonder their inbox was always full. I tried to send it again while underway, no luck. No matter. During the trip, I had received a reply from the custom office, at least one of the messages had landed in their inbox. On arrival, they told us to look for a white building on our starboard side.

We moved deeper into the creek, looking for the white building on the shore. Once we were past the wharf I called them again, they mentioned a dock and instructed us to tie onto it. We hadn't expected to have to dock the boat, from what we'd read online we didn't have to do that, but things change from year to year. The fenders and docking lines weren't ready. I ran around the boat, gathering everything, all the while trying to figure out where that forsaken dock was.

We glimpsed the dock further ahead, it was next to the white building. Getting there meant maneuvering around a few boats, channel markers, and moorings, then we saw two people on the marina dock, waving at us. Docking was always a nerve-wracking experience, we didn't have current to contend with today, but perpendicular to the dock was a large power boat, with nothing between it and our bow. If we came in too fast, we'd bump into it for sure — bumping into still objects is always embarrassing, especially if said objects are worth a lot of money. I was still attaching fenders at this point, while Devine maneuvered Pino around the various obstacles.

As I ran around deck, I heard the Fijian authorities hailing us on the radio again. They couldn't have picked a worse time to hail us, the dock lay ahead and two of the lines weren't yet set up. I ran inside, sweating from all the running — it was also 26°C out, and I was in full foul-weather gear. Couldn't they see that we were right there and that they could ask us questions later?

They asked us to repeat our last port, I gave a curt reply "OPUA!" before darting back outside. We were right near the quarantine dock, I set up the last two dock lines, and threw the ends to the marina staff. We slowed to a stop, our bow drew all-too-near to the power boat ahead. I took note of the name on its side, it was a New Zealand boat, we'd seen it power past us on AIS on the way here.

All went well in the end, Pino was at the dock, and we could relax! The marina staff were friendly, one of the line handlers shook our hand. "Bula! Bula! Welcome to Savusavu!" To which we said "Bula!" Interpreting this to mean, "Hello!" We wished we had taken the time to learn some Fijian before arriving here. Bula could mean welcome, or hello, we hoped for the latter, us repeating "Welcome!" would be a very awkward-penguin thing to do.

We waited for the authorities to visit, and noticed then that the New Zealand power boat had bands of yellow with "crime scene" written on it.

"Is that what happens when they find undeclared vegetables aboard?" We wondered, all the while becoming paranoid about the knob of ginger and pumpkin we'd hidden away. We saw police walking the docks, everyone we'd met so far was all-smiles, but these guys wore grave expressions. They wandered off for a second, I stepped off Pino, moving a short distance away to try and take a photo of Devine and the boat to show others we'd arrived. I took a photo, but a second after, Devine motioned for me to return, a marina attendant was walking towards us as if to say that we had to stay on the boat. With the police walking about, it was best to heed their warning.

It took some time before the authorities came to see us. The first in the long line of officials, was the health inspector. He was a friendly fellow, dressed in casual wear, a colorful blouse and sandals. He had us sign and fill papers — writing down Pino's info and our passport numbers is second nature now. "Is it just you two? Did anyone die aboard this vessel?" To which we replied: "Yes, and no." Important not to get those two answeres mixed up. "Any animals? Insects?" I wondered then. Do sea birds count? At sea, the thought of having a gannet friend always crossed my mind. "No animals aboard, no." Saying this, my mind screamed "gannet friend" over and over again. "Are you sick?" We shook our heads. "We're so healthy." I could see him writing this down now, Rek and Devine: so healthy.

He explained the mosquito situation in Fiji. This country didn't have it as bad as other places, there had been instances of dengue. Ah, dengue, we'd first heard of it while in Japan, there was an outbreak and no one could visit public parks. I didn't know what it was back then, just that it's something you didn't want to get. Since then, we came to be very familiar with dengue because Devine contracted it in French Polynesia. We weren't keen on a repeat, dengue is no joke and the health officer wanted to make sure we understood that. "Fiji doesn't want you sick." We don't want us sick either.

Our second visitor was the quarantine officer, a Fijian of Indian descent, who was very excited to hear that we didn't consume animal products. He admitted to consuming some meat, but aspired to lessen it to none. We had a long conversation about this. He mentioned the health risks associated with it, like in the West, people in Fiji too suffer because of it. We'd almost finished filling out papers, when two Fijian women arrived with more. They were the customs and biosecurity officials. They asked to come inside. The boat was a mess, I went in and shoved everything we had in the v-berth to make room in the saloon. I also set up the table, since it appeared as though we had many more documents to fill — joy oh joy!

The two women gave us the first wave of papers to read and sign, and began to look through our lockers, asking us questions about our stores. One of them took our passports, and began to look through every page, matching the exit stamps with what we'd written on our pre-arrival notice.

"You came from Opua?" We nodded. She looked up at us then. "And before this?" We told her we had stopped in Tonga before New Zealand. "Before this?" she asked again. We enumerated all the places we'd been, Niue, Cook Islands, French Polynesia, Mexico, USA, and then Canada. She looked for the stamps in our passports again. "Where is the Mexico stamp?" Mexico and US don't stamp Canadian passports on entry and exit, it's how it is, but it was hard to make her understand this. The other woman was opening every locker, pulling out bags and asking us questions about their contents. "Captain, what is this?" she said, holding a bag of coffee beans. "Lots and lots of coffee beans." We replied. We had left New Zealand with 6 kg.

Then, the quarantine officer pushed another paper for us to sign, all while making a mess of our galley and asking questions about where we'd been. It was a dizzying experience, having so many people aboard with the table covered in pieces of writing: "Sign this, sign that." While we were doing paperwork, they were exchanging pleasantries (we hope) in Fijian. Devine and I smiled at one another, what a strange scene, sometimes they didn't even pay attention to us at all.

"So, you left Mexico when?" One of the women asked. She was inspecting every inch of our passports and had noticed how the numbers on our passports followed each other, and decided that this was suspicious. It was odd, but it's what happens when you have new passports processed together. "Captain..." The other woman began, she was on her hands and knees, head under the sink. She pulled away, holding the end of a hose with a bottle of mexican dry salsa wedged into it. "What is this?"

In Rarotonga, we had disconnected the water pressure hoses since we no longer had a pressure faucet. We didn't have any plugs around the boat to close them up, but what we did have were many sample-sized bottles of mexican dry salsa, or Tajín. The bottle end was a perfect fit, and we'd since forgotten to replace them with something else.

Devine and I looked at each other, suppressing a laugh, we never thought we'd ever have to justify this crappy fix to anyone. Now we had 3 Fijian officials onboard, all looking at the bottle of salsa en polvo, waiting for an explanation.

"It's dry salsa, from Mexico," we said. They said nothing, and thankfully, didn't ask any more about it, which was good considering there was little more we did want to say. I'm sure they've stumbled upon worse things, or it's possible that all foreign yachts stuff salsa bottles or other oddities up their sink hoses.

We thought they'd only look through the galley cupboards and lockers, but they had decided they needed to look through everything, and we do mean everything. We remembered the pumpkin and ginger root we'd stashed away, one lay under clothes in a bin, the other stuffed deep inside our dirty laundry bag. We hadn't mentioned we had these left-over, because we wanted to eat them — we care deeply about my rhizomes and squashes. We became paranoid. Would they fine us for not declaring them? We could see it now, one of the women pulling a large pumpkin out of our laundry bag, eyes on us, "Captain, what is this?"

They looked in our bilge, under the settee cushions, and in all the bags stacked up in the quarter berth too. All the while, getting nearer to the v-berth, where we our illegal vegetables lived. They looked in the head, and at the plastic bags of medicines we'd inherited from Asma and Herbert from SY Maya. They had not cared to ask about most of the medicines we had, but the woman did find a stack of sealed needles. "Captain, what is this?" This again, we thought. "They're needles." We said simply. "Why do you have so many?" We wished we could remember why Maya had this many so we could repeat and sound like we knew what we were talking about. I didn't know what to say, but no matter, the woman found some other item of interest: our tattoo needles. "It's for tattoos," we explained, showing them a plastic box with our tattoo machine and gear. "Do you like tattoos?" Devine asked. The woman looked at Devine's neck, and at my arm. "No." she said flatly, unimpressed. The other official began to ask me if I knew how to remove tattoos, as if to make sure I knew there were options in case I did change my mind about having them. This was a big contrast to the people in French Polynesia, who gave us a thumbs up as we walked past.

The officials spent a lot of time on Pino, they were pleasant but at this point in the day we were running on fumes. We'd slept 3 hours that night, this, plus the accumulated exhaustion from 15 days of sailing. They left eventually, they had not bothered to check out the v-berth. By then, we'd decided they cared little about our secret stash of veggies. We later found out that the reason they had done such a thorough search was because the owners of the boat that had arrived before us, the one with the "crime scene" tape, were in jail for arriving into the country with undeclared ammunition.

We made lunch, but could not sleep yet. We had fees to pay to the quarantine and health officers and had to go into town to get some money to do it. They told us where to find their respective offices, that Savusavu was a one-street town and that they'd be easy to find. They gave us general direction, we didn't take notes thinking we'd remember, severely overestimating the capabilities of our tired brains. We also moved our boat off the dock and onto a mooring, and pumped some air back into Iggy before going into town.

We were looking for a green building, but none we passed resembled an office. This was our first excursion into town, our first look at Fiji. The city, while small, was very animated and busy, there were people everywhere. Everyone we passed waved at us. "Bula!" We returned their wave and greeting — by now, we knew bula meant hello. We passed by an area packed with buses, with vendors outside trying to sell its passengers soft drinks and snacks. Wild dogs roamed the streets, we passed a green space with people, sitting while chatting and sharing food. We caught a glimpse of the Digicel booth, we'd have to come here later to get some data for our phones. The fresh vegetable market was right next to the bus station, resembling the one in Tonga, but bigger, with stalls selling Indian staples. We couldn't wait to get some paw paw and breadfruit! The town had many stores: lots of hardware shops selling more or less the same content, a hideously pink grocery store called Max Value, a Chinese bakery etc.

We arrived at the edge of town, the part where the street transitions from buildings to bush, and there was still no health or quarantine office in sight. We remembered them saying that the customs office was on the other side of town, where there were fewer buildings. This one, we figured, we couldn't miss. We found it straight away, picked up our passports, and asked for directions to the health and quarantine offices again. They assured us that if we kept walking the other way, that we'd find the green building.

Devine and I dragged our tired bodies back over there, and did find a green building. The quarantine office wasn't on the street, but at the back of it. The quarantine officer took a liking to us, we talked for a long while, but our bodies longed for a rest. We must have said goodbye 10 times before he agreed to let us go. He told us how to find the health office — hopefully this time, we wouldn't forget. We walked out, and about 5 minutes into our walk, we realized that we had contradicting information. "He said it's on the left and past a round-about," I said. "No, no, he said it's on the right close to the customs office." Devine said. I felt my skin getting warmer, I couldn't believe we'd forgotten again! Dead on our feet, Devine volunteered to return to ask the chatty man about the health office again.

We eventually found it, but we forgot something else: they couldn't provide change, and we had to pay the exact amount. The officials had told us this, although neither of us remembered — a feeling all too familiar at this point. We stood there, ready to drop to ground. I considered faking a tear — that's how desperate I was. Our exhaustion must have been obvious, because he took pity on us. He went around the office, and asked other employees for change — what a guy, oooh what a guy!

On the way out, we stopped by the Digicel booth to buy some phone data. I would have preferred we'd go tomorrow, but Devine had insisted on it. We took some amazing shots while becalmed on the ocean during our passage, and Devine wanted to put upload them online. We had heard that the internet in Fiji was cheap, but we hadn't expected to get 25G for 25$, this equated to 15$CAD. In Tonga, all we could get was 5G for 25$(Tongan dollars). We couldn't believe it! The Digicel employees welcomed us to Savusavu, all the while staring at Devine's lip piercings, like they weren't of this earth. We had a chat, and told them we came on a yacht, and right then all the employees turned to us. "Did you hear about the boat with the bullets? Do you know what happened?" The boat with the ammunition was the talk of the town. They asked us about it and we told them what we knew, amused by how fast news traveled here. We left the stall, data in hand, brains melting. We walked past the vegetables market, but we decided not to stop — we'd made enough stops for today. Arriving back on the boat, we prepared dinner, I can't remember what we ate because seconds after eating it we passed out.

We had arrived. We could sleep for more than 3 hours at a time — oh what a feeling!
