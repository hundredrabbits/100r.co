Our navigation rests upon an old CPF 180i Standard Horizon chartplotter, and Navionics (installed onto an old iphone 5s). We use it along with an {AIS} tool(this has nothing to do with AI, it's a radio protocol) for traffic. Navionics lists positions in DMS.

In 2017, Navionics was purchased from an Italian company by American multinational Garmin. Map subscriptions used to cost less than $50 CAD/year(it was below $30 CAD when we first used it in 2016), but in 2025 costs ballooned to $79 CAD/year. If you have the maps downloaded, you don't have to renew the subscription, but you will not receive chart updates. Remember that if you delete maps, you will have to get a subscription to regain access. If you can, use an alternative to Navionics like Open CPN.

The chartplotter was a donation from our friends on SY Dakota (they upgraded theirs and gave us their old one in 2022). For now, it carries all of the c-maps for {western canada}.

GPS and chartplotters are great, they helped make boating safer and easier for many people(us included), but people put undue trust in them. Nowadays sailors often neglect their own observations, or the use of binoculars. The issue with this overreliance is that chartplotters are liable to program errors, the charts may have outdated data or charted innacuracies, and GPS can experience occasional signal failure.

Learning to navigate without GPS and chartplotters helps develop good spatial awareness. Studying routes beforehand, making frequent observations and having multiple backups, will ensure that the boat and crew stay safe. We carry a sextant aboard, and both of us are learning to use it(also, see Celestial Compass). Depending on GPS isn't 100% reliable, and learning to navigate without one might save your life.
